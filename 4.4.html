Cell Potential,the Nerst Equation and Applications:

1. Cell Potential

The cell potential (or electromotive force, EMF) is the voltage difference between the cathode and anode in an electrochemical cell. It determines the ability of a cell to drive an electric current.

E_{\text{cell}} = E_{\text{cathode}} - E_{\text{anode}}

2. The Nernst Equation

The Nernst equation adjusts the cell potential under non-standard conditions (when concentrations differ from 1 M and pressures from 1 atm).

E = E^\circ - \frac{RT}{nF} \ln Q

For room temperature (), it simplifies to:

E = E^\circ - \frac{0.0591}{n} \log Q

Where:

= Cell potential under non-standard conditions

E^{\circ }= Standard cell potential

R= Universal gas constant ()

T = Temperature in Kelvin

n= Number of electrons transferred

F= Faraday's constant ()

Q= Reaction quotient, given by:

Q = \frac{[\text{products}]^m}{[\text{reactants}]^n}

3. Applications of the Nernst Equation:

(a) Electrochemical Cells and Batteries

Determines the voltage of batteries under real conditions (e.g., lithium-ion batteries).

Helps in predicting the discharge rate of a battery.

(b) pH Determination (Glass Electrode)

The pH meter is based on the Nernst equation applied to the hydrogen electrode:

E = E^\circ - \frac{0.0591}{1} \log [H^+]

Since pH = -\log [H⁺], the equation is used to measure pH.

(c) Biological Systems (Nerve Impulses)

The membrane potential in nerve cells is determined using the Nernst equation for ion transport across membranes.

(d) Corrosion Studies

Predicts whether a metal will corrode in a given environment.

Used in cathodic protection to prevent rusting.

(e) Electroplating and Industrial Processes

Helps control electrodeposition rates in electroplating industries.
