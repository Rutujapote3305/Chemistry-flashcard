Particle in a 1D Box - Quantum Model

The particle in a box is a fundamental quantum mechanics model where a particle is confined in a box of length with infinitely high potential walls. The Schrödinger equation for a particle of mass in a one-dimensional box is:

-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = E \psi

with boundary conditions:

\psi(0) = \psi(L) = 0

The solutions for wavefunctions are:

\psi_n (x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right)

where (quantum number).

The corresponding energy levels are:

E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}

where:

h = Planck’s constant

m = Mass of the particle

l = Box length

n = Quantum number (higher means higher energy)

Applications in Conjugated Molecules (π-Electron Systems)

Approximating π-electron behavior in linear conjugated molecules like butadiene, polyenes, and benzene derivatives.

lectrons in conjugated molecules behave like particles in a box, where the length is related to the conjugation length.

The energy gap () between molecular orbitals determines UV-Vis absorption properties:

\Delta E = E_{n+1} - E_n = \frac{(2n+1) h^2}{8mL^2}

Example: The absorption wavelength shifts to longer wavelengths (redshift) as the conjugation length increases, which is observed in β-carotene and polyenes.

Applications in Nanoparticles (Quantum Dots)

uantum dots (nanoparticles of semiconductors) exhibit quantum confinement, where the energy levels are quantized similarly to a particle in a box.

The energy levels in nanoparticles depend on the size of the quantum dot:

E_n \propto \frac{1}{L^2}

Smaller nanoparticles → Larger energy gap → Blue shift in absorption spectra.

Larger nanoparticles → Smaller energy gap → Red shift in optical absorption.

Used in optoelectronics, bioimaging, and solar cells due to their tunable electronic properties.
