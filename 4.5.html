2. Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) Reactions

These involve electron transfer between species.

(a) Oxidation and Reduction:

Oxidation: Loss of electrons ().

Reduction: Gain of electrons ().

Redox Reaction Example:

\text{Zn} + \text{Cu}^{2+} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + \text{Cu}

Cu²⁺ is reduced (oxidizing agent).

Cu²⁺ is reduced (oxidizing agent).

(b) Balancing Redox Reactions (Half-Reaction Method)

1. Write oxidation and reduction half-reactions.

2. Balance atoms and charge using H₂O, H⁺, OH⁻, and electrons.

3. Combine to get the final balanced equation.

(c) Applications of Redox Reactions

Batteries and Fuel Cells (e.g., lithium-ion batteries).

Corrosion and Rust Prevention (e.g., galvanization).

Industrial Production (e.g., electrolysis for metal extraction).

3. Solubility and Solubility Equilibria

Solubility defines how much a substance dissolves in a given solvent at equilibrium.

(a) Solubility Product Constant ()

For a sparingly soluble salt:

\text{BaSO}_4 (s) \rightleftharpoons Ba^{2+} (aq) + SO_4^{2-} (aq)

K_{sp} = [Ba^{2+}][SO_4^{2-}]

(b) Common Ion Effect

Adding a common ion decreases solubility due to Le Chatelier’s Principle.

Example: Adding to AgCl reduces AgCl solubility.

(c) Precipitation and Selective Precipitation

Used to separate ions in solution (e.g., gravimetric analysis).

Basis of water purification and drug formulations.

Applications of Acid-Base, Redox, and Solubility Concepts

1. Industrial Applications: Acid-base catalysis, metallurgy, and electrochemical refining.

2. Biological Systems: Cellular respiration, blood pH control, and metabolic reactions.

3. Pharmaceuticals: Drug solubility and stability in the human body.

4. Environmental Science: Acid rain, wastewater treatment, and soil chemistry.
