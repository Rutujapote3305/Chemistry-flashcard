Electronic spectroscopy

1. Introduction to Electronic Spectroscopy

Electronic spectroscopy studies the absorption or emission of light by molecules as electrons transition between energy levels. It primarily deals with UV-Visible

(UV-Vis) spectroscopy, which examines electronic transitions in molecules.

Wavelength Range: 200–800 nm

Energy Transitions: Involves promotion of electrons from lower-energy molecular orbitals to higher-energy orbitals.

2. Types of Electronic Transitions

In molecules, electronic transitions occur between molecular orbitals:

π → π* (Common in conjugated systems)

n → π* (Common in compounds with lone pairs, e.g., carbonyls)

σ → σ* (High-energy, UV region)

n → σ* (Moderate energy, found in non-bonding systems)

The energy required follows the trend:

\sigma \to \sigma^* > n \to \sigma^* > \pi \to \pi^* > n \to \pi^*

3. UV-Vis Absorption and Chromophores

Chromophores: Groups in molecules responsible for light absorption (e.g., C=C, C=O, aromatic rings).

Bathochromic Shift (Red Shift): Absorption shifts to a longer wavelength (lower energy) due to conjugation.

Hypsochromic Shift (Blue Shift): Absorption shifts to a shorter wavelength (higher energy) due to solvent effects or structural changes.

4. Applications of Electronic Spectroscopy

Determining Conjugation in Molecules (e.g., β-carotene, benzene).

Protein and Nucleic Acid Analysis (UV absorption at 280 nm and 260 nm, respectively).

Metal Complexes and d-d Transitions (Observed in coordination chemistry).
