π-Molecular Orbitals of Butadiene and benzene and Aromaticity.

1. π-Molecular Orbitals of Butadiene (C₄H₆)

Butadiene has 4 conjugated π-electrons over 4 carbon atoms.

The π-electrons are delocalized, forming molecular orbitals through linear combination of p-orbitals.

Hückel Method: The π-system is modeled using the Hückel secular determinant, solving for energy levels.

Molecular Orbital Energy Levels:

ψ₁ (Lowest Energy, Bonding)

ψ₂ (Second Bonding Orbital)

ψ₃ (Antibonding Orbital)

ψ₄ (Highest Energy, Antibonding)

Energy of π Molecular Orbitals (Approximate)

E_n = \alpha + 2\beta \cos \left(\frac{n\pi}{N+1}\right), \quad n = 1,2,3,4

Aromaticity Check (Hückel’s Rule)

Hückel’s Rule: A system is aromatic if it has (4n + 2) π-electrons ( is an integer).

Butadiene has 4 π-electrons (n = 1 does not satisfy 4n+2 rule), so it is not aromatic.

It is still stabilized by conjugation, but lacks full cyclic delocalization.

2. π-Molecular Orbitals of Benzene (C₆H₆)

Benzene has 6 π-electrons, forming a fully conjugated cyclic system.

The six p-orbitals interact to form six molecular orbitals (three bonding, three antibonding).

Delocalization results in equal bond lengths and high stability.

Molecular Orbital Energy Levels:

ψ₁ (Completely Bonding, Lowest Energy)

ψ₂ & ψ₃ (Degenerate Bonding Orbitals)

ψ₄ & ψ₅ (Degenerate Antibonding Orbitals)

ψ₆ (Completely Antibonding, Highest Energy)

Aromaticity Check

Benzene has 6 π-electrons (n = 1 satisfies Hückel’s Rule: 4n + 2 = 6).

It is aromatic, exhibiting delocalized electron density in a cyclic system, leading to exceptional stability.
