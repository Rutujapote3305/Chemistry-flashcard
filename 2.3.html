Fluorescence and its applications in medicine.

1. What is Fluorescence?

Fluorescence is a form of photoluminescence where a molecule absorbs light at a shorter wavelength (higher energy) and emits light at a longer wavelength (lower energy). The process involves:

1. Excitation: A molecule absorbs a photon, exciting an electron to a higher energy state.

2. Relaxation: The excited electron loses some energy through non-radiative processes.

3. Emission: The electron returns to the ground state, releasing a photon of lower energy (longer wavelength).

The time delay between absorption and emission is usually nanoseconds.

2. Fluorescent Molecules (Fluorophores)

Common fluorophores include:

Organic dyes (e.g., fluorescein, rhodamine)

Quantum dots (nanoparticles with tunable fluorescence)

Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP) (used in biological imaging)

3. Applications of Fluorescence in Medicine

(a) Fluorescence Microscopy

Used to image cells, tissues, and biomolecules with high specificity.

Fluorescent tags bind to specific proteins or structures (e.g., immunofluorescence).

(b) Fluorescent Probes in Diagnostics

Fluorescent biosensors detect biomarkers for diseases (e.g., cancer, infections).

(c) Fluorescence in Cancer Detection and Surgery

Fluorescent dyes (e.g., indocyanine green) help visualize tumors.

Fluorescence-guided surgery (FGS) improves precision in tumor removal.

(d) Flow Cytometry

Uses fluorescent markers to analyze cell populations in blood samples.

Key in HIV/AIDS monitoring, leukemia diagnosis, and stem cell research.

(e) Medical Imaging and Drug Delivery

Fluorescent nanoparticles track drug distribution in the body.

PET-CT with fluorescent contrast agents enhances imaging.
