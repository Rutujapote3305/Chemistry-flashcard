Thermodynamic functions:- Energy, Entropy and Free energy

Thermodynamic functions describe the energy transformations in a system and determine the spontaneity of processes.

1. Internal Energy ()

Definition: The total energy of a system, including kinetic and potential energy of molecules.

First Law of Thermodynamics:

dU = dq + dw

For a system at constant volume ():

dU = dq_V

H = U + PV

2. Entropy ()

Definition: A measure of disorder or randomness in a system.

Second Law of Thermodynamics: For spontaneous processes:

dS_{\text{universe}} \geq 0

dS = \frac{dq_{\text{rev}}}{T}

\Delta S = nR \ln \frac{V_f}{V_i}

3. Free Energy Functions

(a) Gibbs Free Energy () (Constant )

Definition: Determines spontaneity at constant pressure and temperature.

Equation:

G = H - TS

\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S

If → Process is non-spontaneous.

If → System is at equilibrium.

(b) Helmholtz Free Energy () (Constant )

Equation:

A = U - TS

Applications

Internal Energy: Used in heat engines and chemical reaction energetics.

Entropy: Important in phase changes, statistical mechanics, and information theory.

Gibbs Free Energy: Determines feasibility of chemical reactions and equilibrium conditions.

Helmholtz Free Energy: Relevant in physics and condensed matter systems.
