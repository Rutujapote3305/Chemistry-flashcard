Principles of spectroscopy and selection

1. Principles of Spectroscopy

Spectroscopy is the study of the interaction between electromagnetic radiation and matter. It provides information about the electronic, vibrational, and rotational energy levels of molecules.

Key Concepts:

Absorption vs. Emission: Absorption: Molecules absorb energy and transition to higher energy states. Emission: Molecules release energy and return to lower energy states.

Energy Quantization: Molecules have discrete energy levels governed by quantum mechanics. Types of Spectroscopy (based on energy regions): Electronic Spectroscopy (UV-Vis, Fluorescence) → Electron transitions. Vibrational Spectroscopy (IR, Raman) → Bond stretching/bending. Rotational Spectroscopy (Microwave) → Molecular rotation.

2. Selection Rules

Selection rules determine which transitions are allowed or forbidden based on quantum mechanics.

(a) Rotational Spectroscopy (Microwave Region) Selection Rule: \Delta J = \pm 1 Nonpolar molecules (O₂, N₂) do not show pure rotational spectra.

(b) Vibrational Spectroscopy (Infrared Region) Selection Rule: \Delta v = \pm 1

(c) Raman Spectroscopy (Scattering-Based) Selection Rule: Requires a change in polarizability of the molecule. Homonuclear diatomic molecules (O₂, N₂) are Raman-active but IR-inactive.

(d) Electronic Spectroscopy (UV-Vis Region) Selection Rules: Spin Selection Rule: (transitions between same spin states are allowed). Laporte Rule: For centrosymmetric molecules, transitions between orbitals of the same parity (g → g or u → u) are forbidden.
