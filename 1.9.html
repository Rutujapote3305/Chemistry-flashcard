Band structure of solids and their role of doping on band structures

1. Band Structure of Solids

The electronic structure of solids is described by band theory, which arises from the overlap of atomic orbitals in a crystal lattice. The two key bands are:

Valence Band (VB): Contains electrons at lower energy levels.

Conduction Band (CB): Higher energy band where electrons can move freely and conduct electricity.

Band Gap (): Energy difference between CB and VB. Determines electrical properties.

Based on band gaps, solids are classified as:

Conductors () → Metals (e.g., Cu, Ag, Al).

Semiconductors () → Si, Ge.

Insulators () → Diamond, Glass.

2. Effect of Doping on Band Structures

Doping introduces impurity atoms into semiconductors, modifying their band structure to enhance conductivity.

(a) n-Type Doping (Electron Donor)

Doping with Group V elements (P, As, Sb) in Si or Ge adds extra electrons.

Creates a new energy level just below the conduction band (donor level).

Electrons easily move to the CB, increasing conductivity.

(b) p-Type Doping (Hole Donor)

Doping with Group III elements (B, Al, Ga) in Si or Ge creates holes.

Introduces an acceptor level just above the valence band.

Electrons jump from VB to the acceptor level, creating mobile holes.

(c) Effect on Band Gap

Doping reduces the effective band gap, making charge carriers available at lower energy.

Intrinsic Semiconductors (pure Si, Ge) → Need thermal excitation to generate carriers.

Extrinsic Semiconductors (doped) → Conductivity increases due to impurity levels.
