Molecular orbitals of diatomic molecules and plots of the multicenter orbitals.

1. Molecular Orbital (MO) Theory for Diatomic Molecules

In diatomic molecules, molecular orbitals are formed by the linear combination of atomic orbitals (LCAO) from two atoms.

The resulting molecular orbitals can be either:

Bonding Molecular Orbitals (Lower Energy, More Stable) → Constructive interference of atomic orbitals.

Antibonding Molecular Orbitals (Higher Energy, Less Stable) → Destructive interference of atomic orbitals.

The combination of atomic orbitals follows symmetry and energy rules:

s-s, p-p, and s-p orbital interactions form bonding and antibonding orbitals.

σ (sigma) orbitals → Symmetric around the bond axis.

π (pi) orbitals → Have a nodal plane along the bond axis.

2. Molecular Orbital Diagram for Diatomic Molecules

(a) Homonuclear Diatomic Molecules (O₂, N₂, etc.)

For second-period diatomic molecules ( to ), the molecular orbitals are arranged as:

\sigma(2s) < \sigma^(2s) < \pi(2p) < \sigma(2p) < \pi^(2p) < \sigma^*(2p)

For O₂, F₂:

\sigma(2s) < \sigma^(2s) < \sigma(2p) < \pi(2p) < \pi^(2p) < \sigma^*(2p)

(b) Heteronuclear Diatomic Molecules (e.g., CO, NO)

The atomic orbitals mix asymmetrically due to different electronegativities.

The more electronegative atom contributes more to bonding MOs, while the less electronegative atom contributes more to antibonding MOs.

Example: Carbon monoxide (CO)

\sigma(2s) < \sigma^(2s) < \pi(2p) < \sigma(2p) < \pi^(2p) < \sigma^*(2p)

3. Plots of Multicenter Molecular Orbitals

(a) Sigma (σ) Molecular Orbitals

σ(2s) → No node, electron density concentrated along bond axis.

σ(2p) → More extended bonding interaction along bond axis.

σ(2p)* → Antibonding orbital with a nodal plane between nuclei.

(b) Pi (π) Molecular Orbitals

π(2p) → Electron density above and below the bond axis.

π(2p)* → Antibonding with a node along bond axis.

(c) Multicenter Orbitals in Polyatomic Molecules

In molecules like benzene (C₆H₆) and ethene (C₂H₄), π-electrons are delocalized over multiple centers.

The highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) determine optical and electronic properties.
