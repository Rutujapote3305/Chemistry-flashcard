Energy level diagrams of diatomic molecules

Molecular orbital (MO) theory describes the energy levels of electrons in diatomic molecules by combining atomic orbitals (AOs) to form molecular orbitals.

These MOs are arranged in an energy level diagram, which shows bonding, antibonding, and nonbonding orbitals.

1. Molecular Orbital Energy Level Diagram for Homonuclear Diatomic Molecules

For second-period elements (Li₂ to Ne₂), atomic orbitals combine as follows:

(a) For (Li₂, Be₂, B₂, C₂, N₂)

\sigma(2s) < \sigma^(2s) < \pi(2p) < \sigma(2p) < \pi^(2p) < \sigma^*(2p)

Example: N₂ (Nitrogen molecule) has a bond order of 3.

(b) For (O₂, F₂, Ne₂)

\sigma(2s) < \sigma^(2s) < \sigma(2p) < \pi(2p) < \pi^(2p) < \sigma^*(2p)

Example: O₂ (Oxygen molecule) is paramagnetic because of two unpaired electrons in π* orbitals.

2. Molecular Orbital Energy Level Diagram for Heteronuclear Diatomic Molecules (CO, NO, HF) When two different atoms form a molecule, their atomic orbitals mix asymmetrically due to different electronegativities. The more electronegative atom contributes more to the bonding MO, while the less electronegative atom contributes more to the antibonding MO.

Example: Carbon Monoxide (CO)

\sigma(2s) < \sigma^(2s) < \pi(2p) < \sigma(2p) < \pi^(2p) < \sigma^*(2p)

Example: Hydrogen Fluoride (HF)

The 1s orbital of hydrogen interacts mainly with 2p of fluorine to form a bonding and antibonding MO.

Fluorine's 2s and some 2p orbitals remain nonbonding due to energy mismatch.

3. Bond Order Calculation from MO Diagrams

\text{Bond Order} = \frac{\text{Number of bonding electrons} - \text{Number of antibonding electrons}}{2}

Bond order = 0 → The molecule does not exist.
